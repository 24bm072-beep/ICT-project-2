What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is when attackers use deception and psychological tricks to make people share sensitive information, give access, or take harmful actions. Instead of attacking computers, they target human behavior.

  • Goal: Bypass security by exploiting trust, urgency, fear, or curiosity.
  • Targets: Anyone — students, employees, executives, even IT teams.
  • Channels: Email, messages, phone calls, social media, and in‑person.

Common Tactics

  • Phishing: Fake emails or sites to steal logins or money.
  • Spear Phishing: Customized messages using personal details.
  • Smishing / Vishing: Scam SMS or phone calls posing as banks, delivery, or support.
  • Pretexting: Made‑up stories (e.g., “IT support” asking for codes).
  • Baiting & Quid‑pro‑quo: Tempting offers (free gifts/USBs) or “help” in exchange for credentials.
  • Tailgating: Following someone into a restricted area without a badge.

Warning Signs

  • Unusual urgency: “Act now or lose access.”
  • Unexpected attachments or login prompts.
  • Sender email or URL looks odd or doesn’t match the real domain.
  • Requests for passwords or one‑time MFA codes (never share these).
  • Poor grammar, strange tone, or messages sent at odd hours from “leaders”.

Quick Defense Checklist

  1. Verify out‑of‑band: If asked to pay or change bank details, call a known number.
  2. Check the URL: Type the site yourself and look for the correct domain and HTTPS.
  3. Use MFA: Prefer app‑based authentication or hardware keys over SMS when possible.
  4. Limit oversharing: Public posts can help attackers craft convincing messages.
  5. Report quickly: Use your school/company reporting method for suspicious messages.
  6. Device hygiene: Update OS/apps and use a password manager.

Real‑World, Safe‑to‑Know Examples

  • Fake delivery notice: SMS claims a package problem and links to a fake site to steal card info.
  • CEO fraud: Email appears from a leader requesting urgent gift card purchases.
  • IT support ruse: Caller says your account is at risk and asks for your MFA code.

Remember: Legitimate teams will not ask for your password or one‑time codes.

A 5‑Step Personal Playbook

  1. Pause: Don’t rush. Urgency is often a trick.
  2. Inspect: Check sender, domain, spelling, tone, and hover over links first.
  3. Validate: Contact the person or company using a trusted number or official site.
  4. Contain: If you clicked, disconnect, change passwords, and run antivirus scans.
  5. Report: Tell your IT/security contact. Fast reporting protects others.
